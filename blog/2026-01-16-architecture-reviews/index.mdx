My first architecture review as a senior engineer was a disaster. I spent an hour presenting a design I'd worked on for three weeks. The principal engineer nodded. Nobody asked questions. The design was approved — and four months later, it failed in production in exactly the ways nobody had questioned. The problem wasn't the design. It was the review process.

## The Failure Modes of Bad Reviews

**The rubber stamp.** The reviewer doesn't have context, doesn't read the document in advance, and approves because "it looks fine." The review catches nothing. False confidence is worse than no review at all.

**The surprise attack.** The designer presents. The reviewer asks questions the designer hasn't considered. The designer gets defensive. The review becomes a debate instead of a collaboration. Everyone leaves frustrated.

**The ivory tower.** The architect reviews alone, writes a list of required changes, and sends it back. The team implements the changes without understanding why. The architecture becomes the architect's design, not the team's.

**The premature review.** The design is half-baked — a few diagrams and some bullet points. The review becomes a brainstorming session. Useful for ideation, terrible for decision-making.

## The Process That Works

### Before the Review: The RFC

Every architecture change starts with an RFC (Request for Comments) document. The format:

1. **Context** — What problem are we solving? Why now? What constraints exist?
2. **Proposed solution** — What are we building? Include diagrams. Not "a microservice" — specific services, specific databases, specific APIs.
3. **Alternatives considered** — What else did we think about? Why did we reject those options? This is the most important section. It shows you did the work.
4. **Trade-offs** — What gets better? What gets worse? Be honest about the downsides. If you can't think of any, you haven't thought hard enough.
5. **Migration plan** — How do we get from current state to proposed state? What's the rollback plan if things go wrong?
6. **Open questions** — What don't you know yet? What assumptions need validation?

The RFC is shared 48 hours before the review. Reviewers are expected to read it and add comments in advance. If nobody reads the RFC, the review is canceled.

### During the Review: 60 Minutes, Structured

- **0-10 min**: The designer presents the RFC. Not reads it — presents the key decisions and trade-offs. Everyone already read the document.
- **10-40 min**: Discussion. Questions, concerns, alternatives. The designer listens and takes notes — doesn't defend. Defensiveness is the enemy of good review.
- **40-50 min**: Decision. Does this design proceed as-is? With modifications? Back to the drawing board? The decision is documented in the RFC.
- **50-60 min**: Action items. Who does what by when? If modifications are needed, what's the follow-up process?

### After the Review: The ADR

The RFC becomes an ADR (Architecture Decision Record) — a permanent document in the repository. Even rejected proposals get archived. Six months later, when someone asks "why did we build it this way?", the ADR answers.

## The Reviewer's Mindset

The best reviewers don't ask "is this design correct?" They ask "what will fail?"

- "What happens when this database is unreachable for 30 seconds?"
- "How many concurrent requests before this service degrades?"
- "What's the blast radius if this IAM role is compromised?"
- "How does an on-call engineer debug this at 3 AM?"
- "What's the cost per million requests? What's the cost if traffic 10x?"

These questions aren't criticisms. They're the engineering work that turns a good design into a production-ready system. If the designer can answer them, the design is solid. If they can't, the review caught something before it became an incident.

## What I Changed After That First Failed Review

1. **No reviews without written RFCs.** Verbal designs are impossible to evaluate rigorously. If you can't write it down, you don't understand it.

2. **Read before, discuss during, decide at the end.** The review is for discussion, not for reading. Reading happens offline. Decisions happen in the room (or on the document).

3. **Separate ideation from decision.** Brainstorming sessions are for generating ideas. Architecture reviews are for making decisions. Mixing the two wastes time and produces half-baked commitments.

4. **Celebrate rejected proposals.** A design that's caught before implementation saved the company months of rework. The designer didn't fail — the process succeeded.

5. **Reviewers share accountability.** If a reviewer approves a design and it fails, the reviewer owns part of the failure. This eliminates rubber-stamping — nobody wants their name attached to a future postmortem.

## The Template I Use

```
# RFC: [Title]

**Status**: Proposed | Approved | Rejected | Implemented | Superseded
**Author**: [Name]
**Date**: [YYYY-MM-DD]
**Reviewers**: [Names]

## Context
[1-3 paragraphs: problem statement, constraints, stakeholders]

## Proposed Solution
[System diagram, component description, data flow]

## Alternatives Considered
| Option | Pros | Cons | Why Rejected |
|---|---|---|---|
| Option A | ... | ... | ... |
| Option B | ... | ... | ... |

## Trade-offs
**What improves**: [performance, reliability, developer experience, cost]
**What degrades**: [complexity, operational burden, migration effort]

## Migration Plan
[Phases, rollback strategy, success criteria]

## Open Questions
- [ ]
- [ ]

## Decision
[Documented after review]
```

Two pages max. If your RFC is longer, you're writing documentation, not a decision document. Split the details into a separate design doc and link it from the RFC.

Architecture reviews aren't about finding the perfect design. They're about finding the fatal flaws before they find you in production. A good review process catches the obvious mistakes, surfaces hidden assumptions, and makes the designer smarter for the next one. Everything else is ceremony.
